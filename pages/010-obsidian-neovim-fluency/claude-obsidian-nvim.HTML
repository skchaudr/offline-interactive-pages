Part 01 · A Translation Series

Neovim, Translated from Obsidian

You already know how to live inside a text graph. This is the part where the graph turns into a terminal.

01

What You Already Know That Transfers

The conventional wisdom about Neovim is that it has a steep learning curve. That framing isn't wrong, exactly, but it's wrong for you. If you've spent years inside Obsidian, you have already internalized most of the load-bearing concepts that Neovim asks you to hold in your head. The terminal interface is unfamiliar; the thinking underneath it isn't. The rest of this artifact is built on that observation, so it's worth being concrete about which five Obsidian habits you can carry across without modification.

Start with linked thinking. Obsidian taught you that a single editor session is really a movement through a graph of files, where every jump — to a backlinked note, to a search result, to today's daily — is a traversal step. Neovim's model is identical in shape, just less decorated. There is no graph view because the graph lives in your head and in your filesystem. The set of files you've recently touched, the path you took to get to the current buffer, the references between notes that ripgrep can find — all of these are graph operations. You already think this way. You'll find Neovim flatters that habit and demands almost nothing extra from it.

Plugin culture is the second free transfer. Obsidian's community is plugin-saturated by design: every workflow problem has at least four community responses, each opinionated and each willing to argue. Neovim is the same way, and for the same reason. A small, sharp core surrounded by an enormous third-party surface is the shape of both ecosystems. The vocabulary differs — “Lua plugin” instead of “community plugin,” LazyVim or kickstart.nvim instead of an Obsidian theme+plugin starter pack — but the cultural disposition is one you already have. You know how to read a plugin README, evaluate maintenance health from commit activity, install something experimental, and uninstall it three days later without feeling guilty. That's a real skill. Neovim users do the same thing, and the muscle for it transfers in one piece.

Search-driven workflow is the third. In Obsidian, the quick switcher is your primary navigation tool — you do not click sidebars to find files; you summon a fuzzy search modal and type three letters of the title. Neovim's analogue is the picker, and Section 4 of this artifact is entirely about pickers, because they are the single most important pattern for an Obsidian veteran to land on. For now, just know that the muscle you have for “press the magic key, type four letters, hit enter” works exactly the same way in Neovim. The picker UI is even visually similar: a floating modal with a prompt at the top and a filtered list below, results narrowing live as you type.

Daily-notes discipline transfers without modification. The keystroke is different and the file lives in a different folder, but the practice — open today's note, capture what's in your head, link out to longer-form notes when something needs more room — works identically. The Neovim plugin called obsidian.nvim, which Section 5 covers in depth, supports daily notes natively; if you have a daily_notes folder in your vault and a template you trust, you can recreate your Obsidian morning ritual in a terminal in roughly two minutes of configuration.

And finally, the command palette. Obsidian's Cmd-P is the universal “I want to do a thing, just let me type what” interface. Neovim has two equivalents, layered. The colon prompt — Ex commands, like :write or :bdelete — is the literal command palette, with a fuzzy-matchable surface. And the leader key, which Section 2 explains in detail, is the keyboard-shortcut layer that maps “press space, then a memorable sequence” onto common commands. Two interfaces, one familiar instinct.

Hold those five — linked thinking, plugin culture, search-driven workflow, daily-notes discipline, command-palette muscle — as the rosetta. The rest of this artifact translates Neovim's unfamiliar surface back into those ideas. When something feels alien, the move is to ask: which of the five is this, in disguise? Almost always, it's one of them.

02

Neovim Vocabulary You Need First

Five terms carry most of the weight in this artifact: modes, buffers, windows, leader keys, and the Lua plugin ecosystem. You'll meet others as you go — text objects, motions, marks, registers — but those are content for later parts of this series. The five here are the ones you need before anything else will make sense, and each one earns a paragraph because the why is the part that makes them stick.

Modes. Neovim has several editing modes, but the four you will live in are normal, insert, visual, and command-line. In normal mode, the default, your keyboard is not a typewriter — it is a sequence of commands. Pressing d is not “type a d”; it's the start of a delete instruction that waits for a noun (a motion or text object) to tell it what to delete. Pressing dw means “delete one word forward.” Pressing d3w means “delete three words forward.” Pressing . after that repeats the entire last edit as a single unit. Insert mode, which you enter by pressing i or a or o among others, is the typewriter — keys insert characters. Visual mode is for selection. Command-line mode is the colon prompt at the bottom of the screen, where you type Ex commands like :write or :source $MYVIMRC.

The reason modes exist is composition. In a modeless editor — every other editor you have used, including Obsidian's editor pane — every shortcut has to coexist on the keyboard alongside ordinary typing, which forces shortcuts into ergonomic edge cases (modifier-key combinations, function-key rows, mouse menus). In a modal editor, the entire keyboard is available for commands when you are in normal mode, which means commands can be short single letters that compose like words in a sentence. Verb plus noun. Operator plus motion. The dot-repeat operator is the clearest proof of this design: it works because every edit in Neovim has a parseable verb-and-object structure, which lets the editor re-execute the last edit as a unit. No modeless editor has anything comparable, and once you have used dot-repeat for a week you understand viscerally why people who use it never want to give it up.

This composability is the inheritance from vi, which Bill Joy wrote at Berkeley in the late 1970s on a teletype connection where character-by-character editing was painfully slow and command-based editing was a survival tactic. Bram Moolenaar's Vim, beginning in 1991, kept the same model and built outward from it for three decades. Neovim, which began in 2014 as a fork aimed at modernizing the codebase, kept the model again. Across roughly five decades, the mode-based composition idea has not been seriously improved upon for text editing at speed. It is genuinely a good design. The learning curve is real, but the curve flattens into something faster than every modeless editor you have ever used.

Why didn't modeless editors win?

They did, mostly. Every editor you've used outside the vi family — VSCode, Sublime, Obsidian itself — is modeless. The trade is real and worth naming: modeless editors are easier on day one because every key types itself, and harder at the upper bound because every command needs a modifier-key chord (Ctrl, Cmd, Alt) to coexist with typing. Modal editors are harder on day one because d isn't a d, but easier at the upper bound because the entire alphabet is free to be a verb. The mode toggle is the cost; the composability is the payoff. People who've used both for long enough often describe modeless editing as “typing with my pinkies on Ctrl forever” once they've internalized modal — not because modeless is bad, but because the ergonomic price becomes legible only in contrast.

Buffers and windows. These are easy to confuse because most editors collapse them into one concept. A buffer is the in-memory representation of a file. A window is a viewport that displays a buffer. The relationship is many-to-many: one buffer can be displayed in two windows simultaneously (useful for looking at two parts of the same long note), and one window can be swapped to show a different buffer without changing the file on disk. When you run :bd (buffer delete), you remove the buffer from memory; when you run :q, you close a window. Closing the last window that displays a buffer does not delete the buffer — it stays loaded in the background, navigable via the buffer picker. This distinction will trip you up exactly once, when you discover that a file you thought you had closed is still hanging around in memory; afterwards it makes sense and becomes a feature rather than a confusion.

Splits and tabs. A split is the act of dividing the current window horizontally or vertically into two windows. :split stacks; :vsplit divides side-by-side. Tabs in Neovim are not what tabs are in your browser — they are layouts of windows, a way to save a particular arrangement of splits and switch between arrangements. For someone coming from Obsidian, a Neovim tab is closer to an Obsidian “workspace” than to an Obsidian tab. Most working Neovim users barely use tabs in practice; they live in splits and use a picker to switch buffers, which is faster than tab-switching and avoids the cognitive cost of managing tab state.

Leader keys. The leader key is a configurable prefix that namespaces your custom keymaps. By long-standing Vim convention, the variable mapleader sets this prefix, and the literal token <leader> in a keymap definition refers to whatever you have set. In most modern community configs — LazyVim, kickstart.nvim, and the broader Lua-era ecosystem — the leader is space. The reason for the leader-key convention is namespacing. Without a prefix, every user-defined shortcut competes with built-in commands for keyboard real estate, and conflicts proliferate. With a leader, you get an entire second-tier menu: <leader>ff can mean “find file” without colliding with the built-in f (find character on line). That's the trick. Leader keys turn the keyboard into a hierarchical menu rather than a flat surface, which is what lets a single config scale to hundreds of personal keymaps without going unhinged.

The Lua plugin ecosystem. Vim plugins were historically written in vimscript, the language Bram Moolenaar created for Vim configuration. Vimscript is functional but slow, idiosyncratic, and hard to write at any scale. Neovim included Lua as a first-class scripting language from the project's earlier days, and the release of 0.5 in July 2021 made Lua a complete platform for plugins by adding built-in Language Server Protocol support and Treesitter-powered syntax to the Lua API. The community pivoted hard. Almost every plugin you will install in 2026 is Lua-based. The plugins you'll meet in this series — Telescope, obsidian.nvim, Snacks, Harpoon — are all Lua plugins, and they form a recognizable family with shared conventions: a config table passed to a setup() function, opt-in keymaps you define in your own config, lazy-loading triggers managed by a plugin manager. Once you have read three Lua plugin READMEs you have effectively read all of them, the way once you've used three Obsidian plugins their settings UIs feel interchangeable.

03

The Translation Table

Each Obsidian habit you have, paired with what does the same job in Neovim, plus one sentence on why the Neovim version feels different even when it's doing the same job. Read this section as prose, not as a lookup table — the “why it feels different” line is the part that actually saves you confusion on day three.

Live · Translation Toggle

Eight habits. Swap the whole view at once.

The teaching is the parallel structure. Toggle between views and notice that the habit (left column) is invariant — only the home changes. That's the entire claim of this section in interactive form: your eight Obsidian habits have eight homes in Neovim, and the verbs feel the same.

Quick switch. In Obsidian you press Cmd-O and type a few letters of a note title. In Neovim the equivalent is a picker command — usually named :Telescope find_files, bound by convention to <leader>ff. The picker opens a floating modal, you type, results filter live, you press enter on the match. The mechanical experience is nearly identical. What feels different: in Obsidian you're searching note titles inside a vault; in Neovim you're searching file paths in a directory tree, which means the directory structure becomes legible to you in a way Obsidian deliberately abstracts. You will start to care about how your vault is organized on disk, not just inside Obsidian's UI.

Vault search. Obsidian's full-text search across the vault has a Neovim analogue called live_grep, bound by convention to <leader>fg. It is backed by ripgrep, the same fast grep tool that the faster Obsidian community search plugins also reach for under the hood. What feels different: Obsidian's search blends title-matching and content-matching by default and ranks both together; live_grep is content-only and ferociously fast on any vault under ten thousand notes. You stop searching titles, because find_files already does that better, and you start searching for the phrase you actually remember writing.

Backlinks. This is the one most Obsidian users worry about losing. Obsidian maintains a real-time index of every link in your vault and shows the backlinks pane for the current note automatically. Neovim does not maintain that index — instead, the obsidian.nvim plugin runs a ripgrep search for occurrences of the current note's filename (as a wiki-link) across the vault when you ask for it, via :Obsidian backlinks. The result is the same list you'd see in Obsidian's sidebar. What feels different: it's on-demand rather than always-visible. You don't see a backlinks pane until you ask, which has the side effect of making the operation feel deliberate, more like a search and less like ambient context. Some users find this clarifying; others miss the at-a-glance count. It's a real trade-off, and worth noticing rather than papering over.

Daily notes. Obsidian's Daily Notes plugin opens today's note, creating it from a template if it doesn't exist. obsidian.nvim's :Obsidian today does exactly the same thing, and there are :Obsidian yesterday and :Obsidian tomorrow as well. What feels different: not much, honestly. This is the most one-to-one translation in the table. The template format obsidian.nvim expects matches the format Obsidian's Templates plugin uses, with the same date-placeholder syntax, which means you can usually drop your existing template files in without modification.

Follow link. Obsidian's “follow link under cursor” is Cmd-Click or Cmd-Enter in most setups. obsidian.nvim's :Obsidian follow_link does the same job, jumping to the linked note or creating it if it doesn't exist. There is also a generic Neovim command, gf (“goto file”), that works on plain filesystem paths but doesn't understand wiki-link syntax — it'll fail on [[Note Title]] because it expects a literal filename. The obsidian.nvim command is the one you want for note-style following. What feels different: the link-following is a deliberate command rather than a mouse gesture, which initially feels slower until you realize you're already in normal mode and the keystroke cost is two characters.

Insert link via [[. Obsidian's [[ trigger pops up an autocomplete list of every note in your vault. obsidian.nvim provides this through a completion source that plugs into nvim-cmp or blink.cmp (the two dominant completion engines in the Lua era), and also through explicit commands like :Obsidian link_new and :Obsidian link. What feels different: in Obsidian the trigger is automatic and ambient; in Neovim you'll usually need to install and configure a completion plugin to get the same ambient feel. The trade-off is that the completion engine you set up serves every other completion you'd want, too — file paths, LSP suggestions, snippets, emoji, buffer text — not just wiki-links. One config, every kind of autocomplete.

Templates. obsidian.nvim has a template system that mirrors Obsidian's, with placeholders for date, title, and arbitrary user-defined variables. You invoke it with :Obsidian template, which opens a picker to choose from your templates directory. Same template files, same placeholders, same result. What feels different: the template invocation flows through a picker, like nearly everything else in modern Neovim, which is a moment that will start feeling deeply familiar by the time you finish Section 4 of this artifact.

Workspaces. Obsidian workspaces save layout state — which notes are open, in which panes, at which scroll positions. obsidian.nvim has a different kind of workspace: in its config you can register multiple vault directories and switch between them with :Obsidian workspace. So an obsidian.nvim “workspace” is closer to an Obsidian vault than to an Obsidian workspace. The vocabulary collision is initially confusing. Neovim has separate session-management plugins (persisted.nvim, auto-session, possession.nvim, and others) for the layout-state idea, decoupled from the vault concept. The two-concept split is cleaner once you adjust to it, because layout-state and vault-state are genuinely different things that Obsidian happened to fold into a single feature.

Underneath all of these translations, a pattern recurs: the Neovim version routes through a picker. Quick-switch picker. Vault-search picker. Backlinks picker. Templates picker. Workspace picker. The picker is the protagonist of Part 1, which is what the next section is about.

04

The Picker Pattern — Protagonist of Part 1

Of every new thing you'll encounter moving from Obsidian to Neovim, the picker is the one that does the most work for you and the one whose shape rewards understanding the most. Once you grok pickers, ninety percent of the search-and-navigate operations you used to do in Obsidian have an obvious home in Neovim, and a lot of operations you couldn't do in Obsidian start to feel obvious too.

A picker is a modal floating window with three components. The top is a prompt: a one-line input where you type. The middle is a result list: a scrollable, fuzzy-filtered set of candidates that updates live as you type. The bottom — or the side, depending on layout — is a previewer: a window that shows the contents of whichever result is currently highlighted. You move through the list with Ctrl-n and Ctrl-p (or arrow keys), preview the highlighted item, and press Enter to act on it. The action depends on the picker — for files, it opens the file; for commands, it runs the command; for git branches, it checks out the branch. That is the entire UI. It is the same UI as Obsidian's quick switcher, generalized to arbitrary sources of candidates.

Watch what stays still and what changes. The chrome — prompt, results pane, preview pane, footer hints — is identical across all four sources. Only the candidates and the preview content swap underneath. That invariance is the abstraction that lets dozens of unrelated plugins reuse the same search modal without inventing their own.

The history of how Neovim arrived at this pattern is worth knowing because it explains why every modern Lua plugin assumes you have a picker installed. The pivotal plugin is Telescope, which emerged from the Neovim community around 2020 and was developed under the nvim-telescope GitHub organization with TJ DeVries — also a Neovim core contributor and the maintainer of kickstart.nvim — as one of its leading authors. Telescope took an idea that had existed in Vim before (fzf.vim, ctrlp.vim, denite.nvim, unite.vim) and rebuilt it as a properly composable Lua framework. The framework introduced a pipeline that has since become the lingua franca of the picker family: a finder that produces candidates, a sorter that ranks them by relevance to the prompt, and a previewer that knows how to display whichever candidate is highlighted. Plug any finder into any sorter into any previewer and you have a custom picker, defined declaratively in a few lines of Lua.

What finder / sorter / previewer actually do

A finder is a function that produces a list of candidates. The simplest finders just shell out to fd or rg --files and return the lines. More complex finders are async generators — they stream candidates in as they're discovered, which is how Telescope can start showing results before the full file walk completes on a large vault. A finder doesn't know how to filter, sort, or display; it only knows how to produce.

A sorter takes the current prompt string and the candidate list and returns a ranked subset. The default sorter is fuzzy: it scores candidates higher when the prompt characters appear close together, when they start at word boundaries, and when matches are early in the string. The fancier sorters use the fzf-native C extension for orders-of-magnitude speedup on big result sets.

A previewer takes the currently-highlighted candidate and renders a representation of it into the floating preview window. For files, it loads the buffer with Treesitter highlighting. For git commits, it shells out to git show. For LSP symbols, it jumps the preview to the line. The previewer interface is the cleanest part of the abstraction — once you understand it, you can write your own previewer for any kind of object in about thirty lines of Lua.

That pipeline is the abstraction that made pickers eat Neovim. Because the three parts are pluggable, the same Telescope core can search files, grep contents, list buffers, browse git commits, jump to LSP symbols, navigate help tags, switch colorschemes, browse keymaps, and approximately a hundred other operations that older Vim users used to spread across a dozen separate plugins. The naming convention reflects this: :Telescope find_files, :Telescope live_grep, :Telescope buffers, :Telescope oldfiles, :Telescope commands, :Telescope help_tags. Each one is a different finder+sorter+previewer combination, exposed as a single Ex command. The mental cost of learning a new one is “remember a new word after :Telescope,” not “install a whole new plugin.”

In community configs the cluster of Telescope commands typically lives under the <leader>f prefix — f for “find.” You get a chord vocabulary that fits comfortably in muscle memory: <leader>ff for find files, <leader>fg for live grep, <leader>fb for buffers, <leader>fr for recent files, <leader>fh for help. The mnemonic is two letters: find+thing. After a few days the chord is faster than thinking about it, and you have replaced everything Obsidian's quick switcher did with a more general, more composable tool that you can extend yourself when you need to.

There is one structural difference between Obsidian's quick switcher and the Neovim picker family that's worth naming out loud, because it's the thing that makes the picker land harder than a beginner expects. Obsidian's switcher is a feature inside Obsidian. The picker, in Neovim, is infrastructure that other features build on top of. When you install Telescope or Snacks.picker or fzf-lua, you are not just installing a quick-switch dialog — you are installing the mechanism by which dozens of other plugins will surface their own pickable interfaces. The LSP integration uses your picker for symbol search and code-action selection. The git plugin uses your picker for branch selection and commit browsing. The diagnostic plugin uses your picker for error navigation. obsidian.nvim, the bridge plugin in Section 5, uses your picker for note selection. You install the picker once and every well-behaved plugin in your config benefits from it without further configuration on your part.

This is a different mental model from Obsidian, where each feature ships with its own search-modal implementation and the styles vary subtly across plugins. In Neovim, search modals are a shared resource. The Lua plugin community converged on this convention deliberately during the post-2021 Lua boom, in part because Telescope's framework made it cheap to do well, and in part because the alternative — every plugin shipping its own search UI — produced visible inconsistency in user configs that everyone agreed was ugly and unnecessary. The convention got formalized in the built-in vim.ui.select escape hatch, discussed below.

Telescope's success spawned a generation of descendants, each making different trade-offs against the original. They're worth knowing about because the choice between them is the first non-trivial configuration decision an Obsidian user lands on when they move into Neovim.

Snacks.picker, by folke (the maintainer of LazyVim, lazy.nvim, which-key.nvim, noice.nvim, and a long list of widely-used plugins), is part of the broader Snacks.nvim suite — a collection of small, tightly-integrated utilities released in 2024 that share configuration and aesthetic. Snacks.picker is faster than Telescope for many operations because it avoids some of the abstraction overhead the framework approach introduced, and it integrates cleanly with other Snacks modules like the dashboard, the bigfile handler, and the scratch-buffer system. If you start with a fresh LazyVim install today, you are likely to have Snacks.picker available out of the box, often alongside Telescope for backward compatibility with older plugin instructions.

fzf-lua, by ibhagwan, takes a different bet: rather than implementing the fuzzy-matching in Lua, it shells out to the fzf binary that Junegunn Choi wrote in Go years before Telescope existed. fzf is one of the fastest fuzzy finders ever written, and the speed difference shows on massive result sets — searching a monorepo with hundreds of thousands of files, grepping a Linux-kernel-sized codebase. For vaults that have grown into the tens of thousands of notes, fzf-lua is consistently the snappiest option. It is also the picker most likely to feel familiar to users who came from terminal fzf workflows before they ever opened Neovim.

mini.pick, part of echasnovski's mini.nvim suite, is the minimalist option. The mini.nvim project's design philosophy is to provide small, dependency-free modules that each cover one concept thoroughly. mini.pick gives you a working picker in a fraction of the configuration surface Telescope requires, with deliberate restraint about scope. If you are the kind of person who reads a 400-line README and wishes it were 80 lines, mini.pick is calibrated for you.

And underneath all of these, Neovim itself ships a primitive function called vim.ui.select. It is the native API that asks the user to pick from a list. By default it falls back to a numbered prompt in the command line — ugly but functional. Any of the pickers above can override vim.ui.select so that when any plugin in your config asks the user to pick from a list, the request is routed through your chosen picker instead of the ugly fallback. This is the escape hatch that makes plugin authors safe: they don't have to depend on a specific picker; they call vim.ui.select and trust that the user's environment has wired up something reasonable underneath.

Previewing is the other piece worth understanding before you install any of these. The previewer in a picker is a real Neovim buffer rendered in a floating window, which means it can do anything Neovim can do: syntax highlighting via Treesitter, LSP hover information, image rendering (with an appropriate plugin), even editing if the picker chooses to allow it. When you fuzzy-search your vault with live_grep and the previewer is showing matched lines with surrounding context and full markdown highlighting, the experience can feel more capable than Obsidian's search-results pane, which is rendered in Obsidian's reading-mode CSS and is read-only. The picker preview is, in a sense, a small editor inside the search dialog, which is the kind of small detail that quietly tips your daily workflow.

The practical consequence for an Obsidian user landing here: pick a picker. Any of the four above. The choice is mostly aesthetic and somewhat performance-sensitive. LazyVim users default to Snacks.picker and rarely think about it again; kickstart.nvim users default to Telescope and rarely think about it again; both are fine starting positions. Once you have a picker, your Obsidian search habits — quick switch, vault search, recent files, command palette — all have homes. The remaining work is wiring obsidian.nvim's vault-aware commands into the same picker, which is what the next section is about.

05

obsidian.nvim — The Bridge Plugin

If pickers are the protagonist of Part 1, obsidian.nvim is the supporting character that ties Neovim's general-purpose machinery back to the specifically-Obsidian conventions you've spent years internalizing. It is the bridge plugin, and it is the single most important plugin to install if you are coming from Obsidian and want your existing vault to keep working without restructuring.

obsidian.nvim was originally created and maintained by epwalsh. Its purpose is to teach Neovim about the conventions Obsidian uses to organize a vault: the wiki-link syntax [[Note Title]], the YAML frontmatter at the top of each note, the daily-notes folder, the templates folder, the conventional places where attachments live, the existence of multiple vaults. None of these are concepts Neovim has out of the box — to a stock Neovim, a markdown file with wiki-links is just text with some funny brackets in it. With obsidian.nvim installed and pointed at your vault directory, Neovim becomes vault-aware: it recognizes wiki-links as navigable, autocompletes note titles when you type [[, can create new notes from templates, and can search the vault using whichever picker you have configured.

That last clause is the important one. obsidian.nvim does not implement its own picker. It has a config option, typically written as picker = "telescope.nvim" or picker = "snacks.pick" or picker = "fzf-lua" or picker = "mini.pick", that tells it which picker installed in your environment to route through. When you invoke :Obsidian quick_switch or :Obsidian search or :Obsidian backlinks, the picker that opens is the picker you already use everywhere else in Neovim, populated with vault-specific candidates. The result is that your “search the vault” muscle and your “search anything in Neovim” muscle become the same muscle, just with different content underneath. This is the single best UX outcome of the bridge-plugin architecture, and it's the reason this plugin matters so much for Obsidian users specifically.

In community configurations — and in the obsidian.nvim README's own example mappings — the Obsidian-specific commands are commonly clustered under the <leader>o namespace, where the second letter selects an action. Common mappings you'll see, presented as illustrative rather than universal: <leader>oo to open the Obsidian command menu (the picker over all :Obsidian subcommands), <leader>oq for quick switch, <leader>os for search, <leader>od for today's daily, <leader>on for new note, <leader>ob for backlinks of the current note, <leader>ot for inserting a template, and <leader>ol for following the link under the cursor. Your config will end up with its own variation; treat these as the pattern, not the prescription.

Each top-level letter is a namespace — a small grammar of what comes next — not a single action. <leader>o doesn't mean “do an Obsidian thing”; it means “I'm about to specify which Obsidian thing.” Two-key chords scale to hundreds of mappings because each prefix limits the next step to a handful of options. Press, then press, then act.

The pattern itself — why a single-letter action namespace, why these particular verbs cluster — is worth unpacking, because once you see why it works for Obsidian operations, you'll start using it for your own keymap groupings everywhere else. The single-letter namespace gives you a memory-anchor: o means “I'm about to do something Obsidian-related.” The second letter is mnemonic on the verb: d for daily, n for new, b for backlinks, t for template, l for link. After roughly a week of use, the muscle memory shifts from “press space, then o, then think about which letter” to “press space-o-d as a chord,” in the same way that pianists eventually stop thinking about individual notes inside a scale. This chunking is what makes leader-key configurations scale to hundreds of mappings without becoming unmanageable: each prefix is a small grammar.

The verbs cluster the way they do because they are the verbs that matter for note-taking specifically. Switch, search, today, new, backlinks, template, follow-link. Look at that list as a complete description of what an Obsidian user does in a vault session and you'll see that it is essentially complete. There are advanced operations — renaming a note and updating its links, tagging a note, working with extended frontmatter, archiving an old note — but the seven verbs above cover something like ninety percent of the operations a vault sees in a week. The namespace is small because the work it represents is small. That's a design choice obsidian.nvim shares with Obsidian itself: keep the verbs few and the surface clean, push everything else into commands you can find when you need them.

A note on maintenance state. Open-source maintenance is fluid; the question of who actively maintains obsidian.nvim has evolved since the plugin was first created, and may evolve again before you read this. If you install it through a community config like LazyVim, the config will pin to whichever fork or version the maintainers consider current; that's usually the right answer. If you install it directly, read the current README for the canonical command list and config schema, because plugin command names occasionally rename between major versions. Treat the mappings in this section as examples of the pattern rather than canonical defaults, because exact bindings are a config-level concern and obsidian.nvim leaves most of them to the user by design.

How which-key.nvim makes the namespace discoverable

The chord-tree widget above is not just a learning toy — it's a near-replica of what which-key.nvim draws on your screen the moment you press a leader prefix and hold for a configurable timeout (default around 300ms). The plugin watches the keymap table you've declared in your config, groups bindings by their common prefix, and renders a floating menu of “here are the next keys that lead somewhere, and here is what they do.”

This solves the discoverability problem that leader-key configs have always had: a flat list of two-key chords is unmemorable, but a hierarchical menu that surfaces itself on demand is browseable. You don't have to remember <leader>od; you press <leader>o, pause, and the menu reminds you. Most LazyVim users have which-key installed by default and rarely think about it, the same way they rarely think about the picker — it's infrastructure that quietly makes the rest of the config legible. If your future config grows large enough to feel unmanageable, which-key is usually the first thing that makes it feel manageable again.

The takeaway, for an Obsidian user landing in Neovim: install one picker, then install obsidian.nvim and point it at your vault. Configure the <leader>o namespace with the verbs you actually use. Within a few days, the muscle memory of “I want to find this note” or “let me open today's daily” will run on Neovim keystrokes instead of Obsidian ones, and you will have ported your most fundamental knowledge work into a terminal without losing the vault, the notes, the links, or any of the years of accumulated structure underneath.

What's Next — Series Map

This artifact is Part 1 of a planned series, and the restraint is intentional. Neovim is famously deep — modal editing alone could fill a book; the LSP integration, treesitter parsing, debug-adapter protocol, async runtime, and configuration ecosystem each could fill another. Trying to compress all of it into a single learning artifact would produce the same shape of failure that “Top 50 Obsidian Plugins” articles produce: comprehensive in scope, useless in practice, forgotten by the reader within an hour. So this first piece deliberately limits itself to the one thing that matters most for an Obsidian user on day one — the picker pattern and the bridge plugin that connects it to your vault — and leaves the rest for later.

The follow-ups in this series will take roughly this shape. The Picker Family Tree will be a standalone piece comparing Telescope, Snacks.picker, fzf-lua, and mini.pick on the dimensions that actually matter for an everyday user: speed on large vaults, configuration surface, integration with other plugins, aesthetic options, and the question of when (and why) to switch between them. Motions and Text Objects, In Notes will introduce the verb-and-noun grammar of normal-mode editing in the specific context of editing markdown — what ciw, dap, and vit actually do when the buffer you're in is a long-form note rather than source code. Different from the general-purpose Vim tutorials, because the editing patterns for prose are not the editing patterns for code, and the existing tutorials almost universally assume code. Harpoon, and the Marks-Stack Idea will introduce Harpoon, ThePrimeagen's small file-pinning plugin: a four-slot stack of “pinned” files you can teleport between with a single keystroke, regardless of where you are in your vault. You'll meet Harpoon early in your Neovim career and it will become one of the keystroke patterns you use the most; it's simple enough to grok on first contact, and it deserves its own short piece because the marks-stack idea generalizes well beyond Harpoon itself.

And then a long-form history piece, separate from the practical guides: The Vim → Neovim → Lua Lineage, tracing the line from Bill Joy's vi in the late 1970s through Bram Moolenaar's Vim through the 2014 Neovim fork through the 2021 Lua-and-LSP renaissance to the current moment. It's a genuinely fun history. Understanding it makes the design choices in your config legible in a way that the practical-only tutorials don't reach, and it gives you context for why the community argues the way it does about plugins and conventions.

Your next move, before any of the above arrives, is to open Neovim and try something. Install a picker. Install obsidian.nvim. Point it at your vault. Press <leader>ff and watch your vault appear in a fuzzy-searchable list. Type four letters of a note title and press enter. You just did Obsidian quick switch in Neovim. That's the foundation, and everything else in this series builds on it. Close the artifact, open the terminal, and try one thing.
