Neovim,
Translated from Obsidian

A rosetta stone for the Obsidian power user who already knows linked thinking, search-driven work, daily notes, plugins, and command palettes — and now wants Neovim to stop feeling like a foreign country.

01

What You Already Know That Transfers

You are not arriving at Neovim empty-handed. If you have spent years in Obsidian, you already understand several of the deeper habits that make Neovim worth learning. You know that a workspace is not only a place where files live. It is a thinking environment. You know that navigation is not the same thing as browsing. You know that a tool becomes powerful when it lets you move by intention: open the note, follow the association, search the vault, jump to the daily log, collect the fragment, return to the trail.

That matters because Neovim is often introduced through the least helpful doorway: a list of strange keys. The better doorway for you is translation. Obsidian taught you to think in a dense graph of markdown files. Neovim asks you to operate inside that same graph with a sharper language of movement and change. The unit is still a note. The medium is still text. The work is still retrieval, linking, refactoring, and recomposition. What changes is that the editor expects your actions to become composable.

The Obsidian habit of linked thinking transfers almost directly. In Obsidian, a link is not decoration. It is a claim that two ideas should remain reachable from each other. In Neovim, that same link can be plain markdown, a wiki link, a tag, a filename, a symbol, or a search result. The editor does not care whether the connection came from a polished graph view or a line of text. It cares that the target can be resolved, opened, previewed, renamed, searched, or transformed.

The search-driven workflow transfers too. Obsidian users often stop caring where something lives because quick switch and global search are faster than folder browsing. Neovim rewards the same instinct. You will still open notes by fuzzy name. You will still search across the vault with ripgrep-backed tools. You will still treat recent files, command history, buffers, backlinks, and symbols as searchable lists. The new word for many of those searchable lists is picker. That word will carry a lot of Part 1.

Your daily-notes discipline also transfers. A daily note is a stable landing pad for thought. It reduces the blank-page cost and gives time a file path. Neovim can keep that pattern, but it changes the surrounding rhythm. Instead of opening the Obsidian app, clicking the date, and writing, you can open the vault in Neovim, ask a command to create or open today's note, insert a template, and continue editing without leaving the keyboard-first flow.

Plugin culture transfers in a subtler way. Obsidian power users already understand that the core app is only part of the instrument. The rest is a curated ecology: plugins, conventions, community defaults, personal taste, and occasionally a weird edge case that becomes part of your craft. Neovim is exactly that, but more explicit. A distribution like LazyVim gives you a starting arrangement; plugins like Telescope, Snacks, fzf-lua, mini.pick, and obsidian.nvim give you specific capabilities; Lua gives the whole thing a configuration language that feels more like software than settings.

Finally, the command palette muscle transfers. Obsidian taught you that the command surface is a memory bridge. You do not memorize every command before you work; you invoke the palette, search for the action, and gradually promote common actions into muscle memory. In Neovim, <leader>? and picker-based command/keymap search play a similar role. The command palette becomes less of a fallback and more of a training wheel that stays useful even after you are fast.

The rosettaObsidian gave you a mental model for a markdown knowledge environment. Neovim gives you a grammar for moving through and rewriting that environment. This artifact is about the translation layer, not the entire editor.
02

Neovim Vocabulary You Need First

The first Neovim vocabulary word is mode. In most writing apps, every key is assumed to insert text unless a modifier key says otherwise. In Vim and Neovim, the editor has different states for different kinds of work. Insert mode is for entering text. Normal mode is for issuing editing commands. Visual mode is for selecting text. Command-line mode is for editor commands. There are more modes, but those four explain most of the initial shock.

Modes exist because vi, and later Vim and Neovim, treat editing as a language. In that language, an operation can combine with a motion. Delete plus word becomes delete word. Change plus inside parentheses becomes replace the contents of the parentheses. Yank plus paragraph becomes copy a paragraph. This is why Normal mode is not a strange punishment. It is the place where verbs and nouns combine. Once you see that, daw is not three random letters. It is a sentence: delete a word.

The famous dot repeat, ., depends on this grammar. If the editor can understand your last change as a structured action, it can replay it. That is why modal editing becomes especially powerful in markdown. Turn one phrase into a link, then repeat the same transformation. Change a bullet shape, then repeat. Wrap one term in backticks, then repeat. Neovim's value is not merely that it avoids the mouse. Its value is that many edits become repeatable units.

A buffer is an open file-like thing in memory. If an Obsidian tab feels like the visible note, a Neovim buffer is the loaded note whether or not you are currently looking at it. A window is a viewport onto a buffer. A split is what happens when you divide the screen into multiple windows. This distinction is weird at first because many apps merge file, tab, and pane into one idea. Neovim separates them. You can have the same buffer visible in two windows. You can close a window without deleting the buffer. You can switch buffers without changing the split layout.

That separation matters for note work. You might keep today's note in one split, a project note in another, and a search result or backlink preview nearby. Moving between windows with <C-h/j/k/l> feels like moving through panes. Switching buffers feels more like changing what document a pane is showing. A picker over buffers is therefore not the same as a file picker. It is a list of already-loaded working context.

A leader key is a prefix for user and plugin shortcuts. In many modern configs, the leader is Space. The idea is not that Space is magical; the idea is namespacing. Plugins need keybindings, users need keybindings, and Vim already has a dense keyboard language. A leader prefix creates a zone for custom actions. Instead of stealing ordinary keys, commands can live under mnemonic groups: file actions under <leader>f, search actions under <leader>s, Obsidian actions under <leader>o.

The leader convention comes from Vim's long tradition of remappable keys and the mapleader variable. The exact history is less important than the pressure it solved: an editor with decades of built-in motions needs a safe namespace for personal and plugin commands. For an Obsidian user, the closest translation is the command palette plus hotkeys. A leader sequence is like a command-palette action that you have promoted into a physical gesture.

Lua is the other vocabulary word that changes the feel of Neovim. Vimscript powered Vim configuration for a long time, and it is still part of the ecosystem. Neovim's modern plugin culture, however, increasingly uses Lua. Around the Neovim 0.5 era, built-in Lua APIs, LSP work, Treesitter integration, and Lua-first plugins made configuration feel more like programming a small editor runtime. That is why modern examples often look like require('plugin').setup { ... } instead of a pile of Vimscript commands.

For you, the important part is not to become a Lua expert before editing notes. It is to recognize that many Neovim plugins expose configuration as structured Lua tables. When you set a picker backend for obsidian.nvim, configure a leader mapping, or change how a plugin displays results, you are often passing data into a setup function. That is software-shaped configuration. It can be intimidating, but it is also why Neovim can become so precisely yours.

One more word: distribution. LazyVim is not Neovim itself. It is a curated starter system built on Neovim and a plugin manager. Kickstart.nvim is another kind of starter: smaller, more educational, more explicit. Your own config is downstream from these. The source of truth for a keybinding claim might be a plugin README, a distribution default, or your personal config. Keeping those layers separate prevents confusion. <leader>ff is a common file-finder shape; it is not a law of nature.

03

The Translation Table

Obsidian's quick switch habit translates to a Neovim picker over notes or files. In Obsidian, quick switch feels like asking the vault, “What note am I thinking of?” In Neovim, the same habit might be :Telescope find_files or an obsidian.nvim quick-switch command routed through your installed picker. The difference is that Neovim treats the result as an editor target: open it in the current window, a split, a vertical split, or sometimes a tab-like workspace depending on the picker action.

Vault search translates to live grep. Obsidian global search gives you matching notes and lines. Neovim often delegates this to ripgrep through a picker, so the search is fast, file-aware, and previewable. In LazyVim, <leader>/ commonly means grep in the root directory, while <leader>sg evokes search by grep. The Neovim version feels different because results are not merely destinations; they are editable entries in a larger workflow. You can send them to a quickfix list, open several, preview context, and return.

Backlinks translate to references. In Obsidian, backlinks are a first-class pane: this note is being mentioned by those notes. In Neovim, obsidian.nvim exposes backlinks as a picker list, while LSP-style reference commands shape the broader mental model. The difference is aesthetic and operational. Obsidian makes backlinks feel visual and app-native. Neovim makes them feel like another searchable set that can be filtered, previewed, opened, and navigated with the same muscles used for files and commands.

Daily notes translate to commands plus templates. Obsidian gives the daily note its own ritual: click, open, type, maybe apply template. In Neovim, the ritual becomes command-driven: open today's note, create it if missing, insert or apply a template, and keep working. With obsidian.nvim, the current community command surface includes daily-note commands such as :Obsidian today and dailies pickers. If your personal config maps this under <leader>od, the gesture becomes your daily landing pad.

Follow-link translates to cursor-sensitive navigation. In Obsidian, following a link is click or keyboard-open. In Neovim, the cursor sits on the link and a command resolves it. The current obsidian.nvim README describes link following and smart actions: if the cursor is on a link, follow the link; if the link does not exist, the plugin can prompt to create it. The Neovim version feels more textual. The link is not a UI object first. It is a piece of markdown under the cursor that the editor can interpret.

Insert-link via [[ translates to completion. In Obsidian, typing wiki brackets opens note suggestions. obsidian.nvim can integrate with completion engines so wiki links and markdown links are suggested as you type [[. The difference is that completion is part of a broader editor-completion system. The same general mechanism that completes code symbols, file paths, snippets, or words can also complete note references.

Templates translate to commands, snippets, and expansion systems. Obsidian templates often feel like document macros: create a note, apply a shape, fill in the blanks. In Neovim, templates can come from obsidian.nvim, from snippets, from Lua functions, from external scripts, or from a simple copied markdown skeleton. The difference is that Neovim does not reserve “template” for one feature. It treats the act as text generation into a buffer.

Workspaces translate to configured vault roots, sessions, tabs, projects, and directory context. Obsidian has vaults and workspaces as app-level concepts. Neovim has current working directories, sessions, project roots, tab pages, buffers, and plugin-defined workspaces. obsidian.nvim has workspace configuration for multiple vaults. The difference is that Neovim exposes more of the machinery. That costs some simplicity, but it also lets you decide whether “workspace” means a vault, a repo, a session restore, a split layout, or a project root.

The deeper translation is this: Obsidian concepts usually appear as named product features. Neovim concepts usually appear as composable mechanisms. Quick switch, backlinks, daily notes, templates, and command palette are not isolated rooms. They are all lists, commands, buffers, text transformations, and pickers wearing different names.

04

The Picker Pattern — Protagonist of Part 1

A picker is an interface for choosing from a list. That sounds almost too simple, but it explains an enormous amount of modern Neovim. A picker gives you a prompt, a set of candidates, a sorting or filtering strategy, a preview, and an action when you confirm. Files can be candidates. Buffers can be candidates. Commands, keymaps, grep results, recent files, diagnostics, git branches, backlinks, tags, help pages, and templates can all become candidates.

For an Obsidian user, the picker is the bridge between command palette, quick switch, search, and backlinks. Obsidian gives each of those features a distinct product surface. Neovim often routes them through the same interaction pattern. You type a few characters, the list narrows, the preview updates, and <CR> confirms the selected item. Inside a Telescope picker, <C-n> / <C-p> or arrow keys move through results, <C-v> opens in a vertical split, and <C-x> opens in a horizontal split.

Telescope became the defining vocabulary for this pattern in the Lua-era Neovim world. Its README describes it as a highly extendable fuzzy finder over lists, centered on modularity, with community-driven builtin pickers, sorters, and previewers. That trio matters. The finder determines what candidates exist. The sorter decides how to rank them as you type. The previewer shows what the highlighted candidate contains. Once you see that pipeline, Telescope stops being “the fuzzy file thing” and becomes a general selection engine.

The README's getting-started example is revealing. It suggests trying :Telescope find_files and shows example mappings: <leader>ff, <leader>fg, <leader>fb, and <leader>fh. Those are not random. Files, grep, buffers, and help tags are four core lists a user constantly needs. The grouping says: put fuzzy access to important lists under a leader namespace so you can invoke them faster than you can remember where anything lives.

LazyVim keeps that idea but evolves the default picker layer. Current LazyVim keymap documentation shows Snacks picker defaults such as <leader><space>, <leader>,, <leader>/, <leader>ff, and <leader>fr. The shape is familiar: root files, buffers, grep, find files, recent files. The exact plugin behind the picker may change, but the user-facing idea stays stable: every important list should be reachable by fuzzy intent.

Kickstart.nvim teaches a slightly different mnemonic. It uses the search namespace for many Telescope actions: <leader>sf, <leader>sg, <leader>sk, <leader>sh, and <leader>s.. This is useful because it reveals the convention rather than one absolute layout. LazyVim often says “file actions live under f, search actions under s.” Kickstart often says “many discovery actions are search actions.” Both are coherent. Both train the same picker muscle.

Snacks.picker, maintained as part of folke's Snacks suite, is one of the modern descendants in this family. It fits naturally into LazyVim because LazyVim already comes from the same ecosystem of polished Neovim tooling. Snacks.picker is not merely “Telescope but newer.” It represents a broader movement toward integrated, fast, cohesive primitives: picker, explorer, notifier, image support, scratch buffers, and other small UI pieces that feel like a toolkit. For a user, the important point is not brand loyalty. It is that the picker has become an expected Neovim primitive.

fzf-lua is another branch. It ties the Neovim picker experience to the spirit and speed of fzf, a command-line fuzzy finder many terminal users already know. The experience can feel leaner and closer to Unix tooling: fast candidate generation, fast filtering, highly configurable actions. If Telescope feels like a modular Neovim-native workbench, fzf-lua often feels like a high-performance terminal-native engine brought into the editor.

mini.pick is the smaller, focused branch. The mini.nvim family tends to provide compact, composable modules with a strong taste for minimalism. mini.pick's value is not that it copies every Telescope surface. Its value is that picking can be a small primitive: choose from a source, filter, preview, act. That restraint is attractive if your config philosophy is “small pieces I understand.”

Then there is vim.ui.select, which is less a picker plugin and more a native escape hatch. It gives plugins a standard way to ask the user to choose from options. A plugin can call the generic selection UI and your config can route that selection through a nicer backend. This matters because it decouples plugin authors from one specific UI. The plugin asks for a choice; your Neovim setup decides how that choice appears.

Now translate this back to Obsidian. Quick switch is a picker over notes. Vault search is a picker over grep results. Command palette is a picker over commands. Recent files is a picker over file history. Backlinks is a picker over references. Templates are often a picker over template files. Workspaces can be a picker over configured vaults or sessions. Once that clicks, Neovim becomes less fragmented. You are not learning six disconnected features. You are learning one interaction pattern applied to six lists.

The picker also explains why preview is so central. Obsidian search gives you context around a match. Telescope and its descendants often give you a preview pane. That preview changes the cost of opening something. You can inspect a note without fully switching into it. You can scan grep results without losing your current buffer. You can evaluate a backlink before jumping. This keeps your working memory intact, which is the actual source of speed.

There is one more subtle reason the picker is Part 1's protagonist: it is the first Neovim abstraction that feels immediately like an Obsidian power feature. Motions, text objects, macros, registers, quickfix lists, jumplists, marks, sessions, LSP, and Treesitter are all important. But the picker is where the Obsidian user says, “Oh, I know this move.” You search, choose, preview, jump, and keep thinking.

Practical anchorOpen Neovim and try one picker. If Telescope is installed, run :Telescope find_files. If LazyVim is using Snacks, try <leader><space>. Your goal is not to memorize every binding. Your goal is to feel the pattern.
05

obsidian.nvim — The Bridge Plugin

obsidian.nvim exists because many people want Obsidian's markdown-vault model without leaving Neovim. The current community-maintained README describes it as a community fork of epwalsh's original plugin, built for people who like the concept of Obsidian but prefer editing in Neovim. That posture is important. The plugin is not trying to recreate the entire Obsidian app. It is trying to make a vault legible to Neovim.

The plugin's bridge role has several parts. It understands workspaces, so multiple vaults can be configured. It understands note creation, daily notes, templates, backlinks, links, tags, and note references. It can integrate with completion engines so [[ produces note suggestions. It can integrate with picker backends — Telescope, fzf-lua, mini.pick, Snacks picker — so lists of notes, templates, backlinks, links, and search results appear through the picker UI you already use elsewhere.

This interlocking is the key. obsidian.nvim is not the picker. It asks for picker behavior when it needs a user to choose something. If your setup uses Telescope, the bridge feels Telescope-shaped. If your setup uses Snacks, it feels Snacks-shaped. The plugin provides the vault semantics; the picker provides the selection surface. That separation is why the same Obsidian action can feel native inside different Neovim configurations.

Exact mappings vary by config and version, so it is better to think in terms of an Obsidian namespace than universal defaults. A common pattern is to put vault actions under <leader>o. The first letter says “Obsidian.” The second letter names the action. <leader>oo can mean an Obsidian command menu. <leader>oq can mean quick switch. <leader>os can mean vault search. The grouping is sensible because it keeps notes work separate from generic file/search work while remaining discoverable through which-key.

Daily-note mappings follow the same logic. <leader>od reads as Obsidian daily. <leader>on reads as Obsidian new note. <leader>ot reads as Obsidian template. The README's command surface includes commands for today, dailies, new notes, and new-from-template, but your config decides the final keys. The pattern is the stable part: put the note-life-cycle commands together.

Graph-adjacent actions also group well under the same namespace. <leader>ob for backlinks and <leader>ol for links fit because they answer the two questions Obsidian users constantly ask: what points here, and where does this point? The Neovim version will usually answer through a picker. That means backlinks feel less like a sidebar and more like a filtered list of navigable references.

The link-under-cursor action is the most literal bridge. In Obsidian, you click or keyboard-open a link. In Neovim, your cursor rests on a textual reference and an action follows it. The current README describes smart behavior around links, tags, checkboxes, and headings. That is the right direction for note editing: make the cursor location meaningful. The editor should know whether the thing under the cursor is a link, a tag, a checkbox, or a heading, then offer an action appropriate to that object.

The reason the README and community examples tend to group these mappings is not aesthetic. It is retrieval. You should not need to remember fifteen isolated gestures. You remember the namespace, press leader-o, and let which-key or your memory finish the command. That is Obsidian command-palette thinking translated into leader-key structure. First discoverable, then mnemonic, then automatic.

There is a clean boundary here. Obsidian remains excellent at mobile sync, graph visualization, and app-level experiences Neovim does not need to copy. Neovim becomes excellent at editing, navigation, refactoring, local automation, and keyboard-native note work. obsidian.nvim is the handshake. It lets your markdown vault become a serious editing environment without pretending that the Obsidian app and Neovim are the same product.

06

What's Next — Series Map

This artifact is Part 1. The restraint is intentional. Neovim is too large to explain honestly in one page, and an Obsidian user does not need an encyclopedia on first contact. The first move is translation: understand that your existing power-user habits already map onto buffers, commands, leader namespaces, pickers, and vault-aware plugins.

The next artifact should go deeper into the picker family tree. Telescope deserves its own treatment because picker, finder, sorter, previewer, and action form one of the clearest architectures in modern Neovim. Snacks.picker, fzf-lua, mini.pick, and vim.ui.select deserve comparison not as a shopping guide, but as different answers to the same interface question: how should the editor let you choose from a meaningful list?

After that, motions and text objects should be translated in the context of editing notes, not toy examples. The interesting question is not “what does ciw mean?” The interesting question is how to turn messy markdown into clean linked notes with operations that compose and repeat. That is where dot-repeat, surround, paragraphs, headings, links, and visual selections become practical.

You will also meet Harpoon, and it is simple enough to grok on first contact. Harpoon is basically the marks-stack idea made project-practical: keep a small set of important files close, then jump between them without searching every time. For an Obsidian user, think of it as a tiny working set pinned above the larger vault.

A separate history piece should cover Vim to Neovim to Lua. That lineage matters because it explains why the editor contains old keys, modern APIs, modal grammar, plugin ecosystems, and Lua configuration all at once. Without the history, Neovim looks inconsistent. With the history, it looks layered.

Your next move is concrete: open Neovim inside a markdown vault and invoke one picker. Find a note. Grep the vault. Open a recent file. Then follow one link or create today's daily note. Do not try to become fluent by reading about fluency. Touch the system. Let one familiar Obsidian habit land in Neovim, then let the editor teach you the next move.

Source posture: keybinding chips distinguish plugin README examples, LazyVim/kickstart conventions, current obsidian.nvim command surfaces, and common user namespace patterns. Exact downstream mappings remain config-dependent.
